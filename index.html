Can India become rich before it grows old?

No

(as of April 04, 2025)

India faces significant challenges in achieving the required growth rates to become a rich country before its demographic peak in 2035. The projected nominal GDP growth rate of 7.0% falls short of the needed 18.0% annual increase to reach the rich country threshold of $13,000 nominal per capita by 2035. Additionally, structural constraints such as persistent food inflation, stagnant job growth, and a rising trade deficit hinder India's ability to sustain high growth rates.

What Is Going Wrong

  1. India's GDP growth rate is projected at 6.5% for 2025, which is below the required 8% needed to achieve developed status by 2047.
  2. Only about 20% of India's workforce is employed in the formal sector, with the majority in informal jobs, indicating a significant gap in formal job creation.
  3. Manufacturing contributes about 25% to India's GDP, which is lower than the 30-40% seen in many developed economies, indicating a need for increased productivity and sectoral contribution.
  4. Public capital expenditure (capex) has been subdued, with utilization rates falling in key states, hindering infrastructure development necessary for sustained growth.
  5. Despite a large workforce, over 44% of India's workforce is engaged in agriculture, while only 31% work in services and 25% in industry, highlighting a structural imbalance in human capital utilization.

The RBI's recent policies have helped India navigate global economic uncertainties while maintaining strong macroeconomic fundamentals.

  3. Nirmala Sitharaman (Minister of Finance) - Sitharaman's role as Finance Minister gives her significant influence over fiscal policies, including budget allocations and tax reforms. Her efforts in balancing fiscal consolidation with growth objectives have been crucial in sustaining India's economic momentum. The Union Budget 2025-26, under her stewardship, focuses on capital expenditure and boosting household incomes, contributing to India's sustained growth.

  4. Parameswaran Iyer (CEO, NITI Aayog) - As the CEO of NITI Aayog, Iyer is responsible for implementing key policy initiatives and reforms aimed at enhancing India's economic competitiveness. His role involves coordinating with various ministries to drive transformative changes, which are critical for addressing binding constraints and ensuring sustainable growth.

  5. Rajiv Kumar (Vice Chairman, NITI Aayog) - Kumar's position as Vice Chairman of NITI Aayog allows him to shape strategic policy decisions and reforms. He plays a key role in advising the government on economic matters, contributing to the development of policies that address critical economic bottlenecks and drive growth. His expertise is crucial in guiding India's economic trajectory towards becoming a major global player.
