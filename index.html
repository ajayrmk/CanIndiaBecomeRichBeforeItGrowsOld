Can India become rich before it grows old?

No

(as of April 21, 2025)

India is unlikely to become rich before it grows old because the required nominal annual growth rate of 18.0% to reach the rich country threshold of $13,000 nominal GNI per capita within the next 10 years far exceeds the projected nominal growth rate of about 7.0% per year. Similarly, the required PPP growth rate of 5.9% slightly surpasses the projected 5.0% growth rate, indicating a persistent shortfall in achieving the income thresholds before demographic peak in 2035.

What Is Going Wrong

  1. India's GDP growth rate slowed to 6.5% in fiscal year 2024-25 from 9.2% in 2023-24, below the 8%+ sustained growth rate generally considered necessary to achieve rapid income convergence with advanced economies.
  2. Manufacturing contributes about 16-17% to India's GDP, significantly below the 25-30% share typical of rapidly industrializing economies needed to generate sufficient quality jobs and productivity gains.
  3. Formal sector job creation remains inadequate, with only about 10-12% of the workforce employed in formal jobs, while over 80% remain in informal employment lacking social security and productivity benefits; this is far below the 30-40% formalization needed for inclusive growth.
  4. Labor productivity in India is approximately 28% of the US level and about 40-50% of China’s, indicating a large productivity gap that constrains income growth and competitiveness.
  5. Infrastructure investment in India is around 7% of GDP, below the 10-12% threshold recommended by NITI Aayog and the World Bank to support sustained economic growth and urbanization.

Key Individuals Responsible

  1. Narendra Modi (Prime Minister of India)
    As the head of government, Narendra Modi holds the highest scope of authority in shaping India's economic trajectory, directly influencing policy formulation and implementation across all sectors. Under his leadership, India doubled its GDP from USD 1 trillion in 2015 to USD 3 trillion in 2025, achieving a 105% growth rate unmatched by any other major economy globally, reflecting his decisive role in structural reforms, ease of doing business improvements, and economic expansion. His administration's focus on digital transformation, infrastructure, and global trade integration directly impacts the scale of economic growth and job creation for over 5 billion people.

  2. Nirmala Sitharaman (Union Minister of Finance and Corporate Affairs)
    Nirmala Sitharaman oversees fiscal policy, budgetary allocations, and financial regulations critical to India's economic health. Her stewardship has been pivotal in maintaining macroeconomic stability, managing inflation, and promoting investment, which supports the IMF's 5% growth forecast for India in She commands significant implementation power through budget execution and reforms in taxation and public expenditure, directly influencing the binding constraints of capital availability and fiscal discipline essential for sustained growth.

  3. Sanjay Malhotra (Governor, Reserve Bank of India)
    As the RBI Governor, Sanjay Malhotra controls monetary policy, banking regulation, and financial sector stability, which are key bottlenecks for India's growth. His recent tenure has focused on balancing inflation control with credit expansion to support economic activity, thereby influencing interest rates and liquidity conditions that affect millions of businesses and consumers. The RBI's role in managing currency stability and financial inclusion under his leadership is critical to enabling investment and consumption growth.

  4. Parameswaran Iyer (CEO, NITI Aayog)
    Parameswaran Iyer leads India's premier policy think tank responsible for strategic planning and coordination of economic reforms and development programs. His influence extends to addressing structural constraints such as infrastructure deficits, skill development, and innovation ecosystems that underpin India's demographic dividend. NITI Aayog's data-driven policy recommendations and implementation oversight have contributed to measurable improvements in sectors like healthcare, digital economy, and manufacturing, impacting millions of citizens and businesses.

  5. Ashwini Vaishnaw (Minister of Railways, Communications and Electronics & IT)
    Ashwini Vaishnaw manages critical infrastructure sectors that serve as growth multipliers for the Indian economy. His portfolio includes the modernization of railways, expansion of digital connectivity, and promotion of electronics manufacturing, which are essential for reducing logistical bottlenecks and enhancing productivity. Recent initiatives under his leadership have accelerated digital transformation and improved supply chain efficiency, directly affecting economic output and employment across urban and rural India.
