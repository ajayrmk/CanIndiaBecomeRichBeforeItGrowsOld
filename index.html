Can India become rich before it grows old?

No

(as of April 30, 2025)

India faces significant challenges in achieving the required growth rates to become rich before its demographic peak. The projected nominal GDP growth rate of about 7.0% is far below the required annual growth rate of 17.7% to reach the rich country threshold of $13,000 nominal per capita by 2035. Additionally, structural constraints such as providing productive jobs for a rising working-age population and navigating geopolitical uncertainties will impact growth.

What Is Going Wrong

  1. India's GDP growth rate is 8.2% in FY23/24, which is high but needs to be sustained at higher levels to achieve rapid economic transformation. For instance, maintaining a growth rate above 9% consistently could be crucial for catching up with developed economies.
  2. Despite a strong growth rate, urban unemployment remains high, especially among youth, at 16.8% in FY24/25. This indicates a need for more effective job creation strategies to match the growing workforce.
  3. The manufacturing sector grew by 9.9% in FY23/24, but its contribution to GDP is not as high as in other major economies. For example, manufacturing contributes about 16% to India's GDP, compared to over 30% in countries like China.
  4. Public investment in infrastructure has been a growth driver, but there is still a significant gap in achieving the desired level of infrastructure development. For instance, India aims to spend about 10% of its GDP on infrastructure annually, but current spending is below this target.
  5. The female labor force participation rate in India is around 20%, significantly lower than the global average. Increasing this rate to at least 40% could significantly boost economic growth by leveraging a larger workforce.

  1. Nirmala Sitharaman (Minister of Finance, Government of India) As the Minister of Finance, Nirmala Sitharaman plays a crucial role in shaping India's economic policies. Her decisions influence fiscal policies, which have a direct impact on India's growth trajectory. For instance, her budgetary allocations can affect sectors like infrastructure and education, which are vital for sustained economic growth. India's projected growth rate of 2% in 2025 underscores the importance of her fiscal management.

  2. Sanjay Malhotra (Governor, Reserve Bank of India) As the RBI Governor, Sanjay Malhotra has significant influence over monetary policy, which affects inflation, interest rates, and overall economic stability. His decisions on interest rates and liquidity management can impact borrowing costs and investment decisions across various sectors, thereby influencing India's economic growth.

  3. Parameswaran Iyer (CEO, NITI Aayog) As the CEO of NITI Aayog, Parameswaran Iyer is instrumental in formulating and implementing policies that drive India's economic development. His role involves coordinating with various ministries to ensure policy coherence and effective implementation, which is crucial for addressing binding constraints and enhancing economic growth.

  4. Piyush Goyal (Minister of Commerce and Industry, Government of India) Piyush Goyal, as the Minister of Commerce and Industry, plays a pivotal role in shaping India's trade policies and promoting economic growth through trade expansion. His efforts to deepen integration with global supply chains and enhance trade agreements can significantly impact India's export-led growth and job creation.

  5. Droupadi Murmu (President of India) As the President of India, Droupadi Murmu holds ceremonial and constitutional powers that can influence policy directions through her role in appointing key officials and signing legislation. While her direct economic impact may be less immediate compared to other officials, her influence on the political environment can indirectly affect economic stability and growth.
