Can India become rich before it grows old?

No

(as of April 16, 2025)

India faces significant challenges in achieving the required growth rates to become rich before its demographic peak in 2035. The projected nominal GDP growth rate of 7.0% falls short of the needed 18.0% annual increase to reach the rich country threshold of $13,000 nominal per capita by 2035. Additionally, structural constraints such as stagnant job growth, persistent food inflation, and a rising trade deficit hinder India's economic progress.

What Is Going Wrong

  1. India's GDP growth rate is projected to be around 7% in the medium term, which is below the 8-10% required for rapid economic transformation to achieve high-income status by 2047.
  2. Despite a large workforce, India faces jobless economic growth, with workforce productivity being one of the lowest globally, and unemployment among urban youth remains elevated at 16.8%.
  3. India's workforce productivity is significantly lower than global averages, contributing to inefficient economic output despite having the world's second-largest labor force.
  4. India's infrastructure spending is crucial for growth, but it faces challenges in improving logistics and construction efficiency, ranking poorly in dealing with construction permits.
  5. India's social welfare spending is only about 8.6% of GDP, much lower than the OECD average, and child malnutrition remains high, affecting human capital development.

  1. Nirmala Sitharaman (Minister of Finance, Government of India) As the Minister of Finance, Sitharaman plays a crucial role in shaping India's economic policies, including budgetary allocations and fiscal reforms. Her decisions significantly influence the country's growth trajectory, with recent budgets focusing on infrastructure development and digital transformation. For instance, the government's emphasis on infrastructure under the PM GatiShakti National Master Plan aims to boost economic growth by enhancing connectivity and efficiency.

  2. Sanjay Malhotra (Governor, Reserve Bank of India) As the RBI Governor, Malhotra oversees monetary policy, influencing inflation rates and interest rates, which are critical for maintaining economic stability. His decisions impact the entire financial sector, affecting borrowing costs and investment decisions across industries. The RBI's recent adjustments in interest rates have been aimed at managing inflation while supporting growth.

  3. Ashwini Vaishnaw (Minister of Railways and IT, Government of India) Vaishnaw's dual role in railways and IT positions him to drive infrastructure development and digital transformation, both of which are vital for India's economic growth. His initiatives in these sectors can enhance connectivity, improve efficiency, and foster innovation, contributing to the country's competitiveness in global markets.

  4. Jyotiraditya Scindia (Minister of Civil Aviation, Government of India) As the Minister of Civil Aviation, Scindia's efforts to expand and modernize India's aviation sector can significantly boost economic growth by enhancing connectivity and facilitating trade. His initiatives in this area contribute to improving infrastructure, which is essential for sustaining high growth rates and integrating India into global supply chains.

  5. Suman Bery (Vice Chairman, NITI Aayog) Bery's role at NITI Aayog involves advising on policy reforms and strategic initiatives aimed at fostering inclusive and sustainable growth. His influence extends to key sectors such as healthcare, education, and infrastructure, where policy interventions can have a broad impact on India's economic trajectory and demographic dividend.
