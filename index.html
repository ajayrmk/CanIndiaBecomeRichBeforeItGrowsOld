Can India become rich before it grows old?

Unknown

(as of April 23, 2025)

No. India faces a critical growth-rate shortfall against required thresholds, with nominal GNI per capita needing 18% annual growth to reach $13,000 by 2035 but achieving only 7% projected growth, compounded by structural constraints in job creation and productivity.

Growth Rate Analysis

  • Nominal GNI per capita gap: Current $2,480.8 requires 18% annual growth to reach $13,000 by 2035, but projections show 7% nominal growth (implied from real GDP growth of 5.9–7.3% plus ~1–2% inflation).
  • PPP-adjusted gap: Requires 5.9% annual growth but achieves 5.0% (based on real GDP trends), extending the timeline beyond demographic peak.

Structural Constraints

  1. Labor market: UBS notes insufficient "productive jobs for the rising working-age population," risking underutilization of demographic dividend.
  2. Export competitiveness: Deloitte highlights "geopolitical uncertainties and delayed Western recovery" dampening export-led growth.
  3. Productivity: Manufacturing and digitalization gains (projected 6.5% potential growth) remain below the 8–9% required to offset demographic pressures.

Demographic Timeline

  • Peak working-age population: 2035, leaving 10 years to achieve high-income status.
  • Per-capita growth requirement: $1,052 annual nominal GNI increase (vs. $174 at current 7% growth), necessitating unprecedented policy interventions.

Policy Impacts

  • Fiscal stimulus: Deloitte cites "significant uptick in government spending" driving 2025 growth to 6.5–6.8%, insufficient for GNI leap.
  • Inflation management: IMF projects 4.2% CPI in 2025, allowing moderate monetary easing but insufficient to bridge growth gap.

Conclusion

India’s 5.9–7.3% real GDP growth translates to 7–9% nominal GNI growth, falling short of the 18% required. Without radical productivity acceleration or demographic rebalancing, India risks aging at middle-income status.

What Is Going Wrong

  1. Current FY2024–25 GDP growth projected at 6.4%, below the 8%+ required to achieve high-income status by 2047 (MoSPI/NITI Aayog targets).
  2. Youth unemployment at 15%, with only 25% workforce in industry versus the 35–40% needed for manufacturing-led growth (World Bank structural benchmarks).
  3. 44% of workforce contributes 18–20% of GDP (MoSPI data), indicating labor productivity 3–4× lower than services/industry sectors.
  4. Secondary sector growth below 6% (vs. 10–12% required for "Make in India" goals), contributing ~16% of GDP against 25% East Asian peer benchmarks (RBI reports).
  5. Skilled labor participation below 5% of workforce (NITI Aayog 2023 data), with vocational training coverage at 2–4% versus China’s 15–20% (World Bank comparisons).
