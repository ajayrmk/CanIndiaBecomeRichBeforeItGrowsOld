Can India become rich before it grows old?

No

(as of April 05, 2025)

India faces significant challenges in achieving the required growth rates to become rich before its demographic peak. The projected nominal GDP growth rate of around 7.0% falls short of the needed 18.0% annual increase to reach the rich country threshold by 2035. Additionally, structural constraints such as stagnant job growth, persistent food inflation, and a rising trade gap hinder India's ability to accelerate its economic expansion.

What Is Going Wrong

  1. India's GDP growth rate averages over 6.5% between 2016-17 and 2022-23, but to achieve rapid economic transformation, a sustained growth rate closer to 8-10% is often considered necessary for significant wealth creation.
  2. Despite adding 170 million jobs between 2016-17 and 2022-23, India's unemployment rate remains around 8.2% as of January 2025, indicating a need for more robust job creation to match population growth.
  3. The labour-capital ratio in India's services sector is 1.17, suggesting low labour intensity, which may hinder job creation in this sector. For comparison, more labour-intensive sectors are needed to absorb the workforce effectively.
  4. India aims to increase its capital investment outlay to Rs 10 lakh crore in the 2023-24 budget, which is about 3.3% of GDP, but this is still below the levels seen in countries with rapid infrastructure development, such as China, which has historically invested over 8% of GDP in infrastructure.
  5. India's female labor participation rate, while increasing, remains significantly lower than in many developed economies. For instance, the female labor force participation rate in India is around 25%, compared to over 50% in many OECD countries, indicating a substantial untapped workforce.

  2. Sanjay Malhotra (Governor, Reserve Bank of India) - As the RBI Governor, Malhotra plays a crucial role in monetary policy decisions, influencing inflation and interest rates. His recent actions, such as cutting interest rates, aim to boost a slowing economy, which is critical for maintaining growth momentum. The RBI's policies under his leadership will be pivotal in managing India's economic challenges, including food inflation and stagnant job growth.

  3. Nirmala Sitharaman (Minister of Finance) - Sitharaman's role as Finance Minister gives her significant influence over fiscal policies, including tax reforms and budget allocations. Her recent budgets have focused on tax cuts and infrastructure spending, which are crucial for sustaining economic growth and attracting foreign investment. Her policies will be key in addressing structural challenges such as trade deficits and sluggish manufacturing.

  4. Ashwini Vaishnaw (Minister of Railways and IT) - Vaishnaw's dual role in railways and IT places him at the forefront of infrastructure development and digital transformation. His emphasis on combining manufacturing with high-value services aligns with India's strategy to move beyond low-skilled manufacturing, which is essential for capturing more value in global supply chains. His initiatives in IT and infrastructure will be vital for sustaining growth and creating jobs.

  5. Suman Bery (Vice Chairman, NITI Aayog) - As Vice Chairman of NITI Aayog, Bery plays a pivotal role in policy formulation and strategic planning for India's economic development. His involvement in shaping policies related to inclusive growth, infrastructure development, and innovation will be crucial in addressing India's demographic dividend and ensuring sustainable economic expansion. NITI Aayog's initiatives under his guidance will focus on leveraging technology and manufacturing to drive growth.
